Why can stacked circles draw anything?

One rotating vector traces a circle. Attach a second vector to the tip of the first — spinning faster or slower — and the combined tip carves a richer curve: lobes, loops, even sharp cusps. Stack a third, a fourth, and every extra term adds finer detail.

The Fourier claim is that with enough of these stacked rotations, at the right speeds and starting phases, you can retrace any closed path exactly. The DFT is just the recipe that reads your drawing and tells you each circle's radius (|Xk|), starting angle (φk), and rotation speed (k cycles per trip around the path).

Beyond the visualizer

Four flavours of Fourier — and the FFT that makes them fast

FT FS DTFT DFT

The canvas above runs a DFT every frame. "Fourier transform" actually names a family of four related transforms, one for each way your signal can be continuous or discrete in time and one-shot or repeating.

In plain English — what is a Fourier transform?

Imagine a fruit smoothie. You can taste banana, strawberry, and milk, but they're all blended together. A Fourier transform is like a magic un-blender: you pour in the smoothie (a sound, a wiggly line, a photo) and it hands you back the separate ingredients — each one a pure sine wave at a specific speed.

The "ingredients" are called frequencies. Low frequencies wiggle slowly (a bass drum); high frequencies wiggle quickly (a whistle). Every complicated signal in the world — a heartbeat, a photo, your voice — is really just a pile of these pure-tone ingredients added together. The Fourier transform tells you exactly how much of each ingredient is in the mix.

Quick glossary before we dive in:

  • Signal — anything that changes over time (or space, for an image). A sound wave, a stock price, a line you drew.
  • Frequency — how many times something wiggles per second. Measured in hertz (Hz). Middle C on a piano is 261 Hz; a mosquito's wings are ~600 Hz.
  • Amplitude — how big a wiggle is. Loud sounds have big amplitude; quiet sounds are small.
  • Phase — where a wave starts. Two waves of the same frequency can be "in sync" or "offset" from each other.
  • Spectrum — the list of all frequencies in a signal, with how much of each. It's the Fourier transform's output.
  • DFT / FFT — the computer versions. DFT is the math; FFT is a clever fast way to compute it.

The duality: whenever one domain is discrete, the other becomes periodic. Sample in time → periodic spectrum. Periodic in time → discrete spectrum. Four quadrants fall out of that rule.

Continuous · Aperiodic

Fourier Transform FT

Kid version: you clap once in a quiet room. The sound is smooth (not chopped up) and doesn't repeat. The FT is the un-blender for this kind of sound — it tells you all the pitches that made up that single clap.

The classic, "general" Fourier transform. Takes an arbitrary continuous signal and decomposes it into a continuous spectrum of frequencies.

Picture: a single clap in a quiet room — one-off, analogue, everything is smooth.

Continuous · Periodic

Fourier Series FS

Kid version: you play a note on a violin and hold it. The sound repeats the same wiggle over and over. The un-blender here finds a ladder of ingredients: the main pitch, then twice that pitch, three times, and so on. Those are called harmonics.

A continuous signal that repeats every T seconds is built only from integer multiples of the fundamental frequency. The spectrum becomes a discrete ladder of harmonics.

Picture: a violin note held steady — periodic analogue waveform, harmonics at 1×, 2×, 3× … the fundamental.

Discrete · Aperiodic

Discrete-Time FT DTFT

Kid version: a microphone writes down the sound as dots (samples), one dot every tiny moment. Imagine it records forever. The DTFT is the un-blender for that endless dotted stream. Nobody actually computes this — it has infinite dots — but it's useful as a mental model.

The signal is sampled at integer times but never repeats. The output is continuous in frequency — and it's also periodic with period 2π (sampling folds frequencies back on themselves).

Picture: an endless stream of microphone samples you analyse as a theoretical object. You wouldn't compute it directly — it's infinite.

Discrete · Periodic

Discrete Fourier Transform DFT

Kid version: grab a short clip of sound — say 1024 dots. Pretend those dots repeat forever. Now your un-blender has a finite job: 1024 dots in, 1024 frequency-bin amounts out. This is the only flavour a computer can actually calculate — and it's what the canvas above runs on every frame of your drawing.

The one a computer actually computes. Take N samples, assume they repeat, and get back N frequency bins. Finite in both domains — perfect for a CPU.

Picture: this visualizer! Every frame of the epicycle animation above comes straight from a DFT of your drawing.

Why the FFT is a big deal

Kid version: imagine sorting 1000 playing cards by number. The slow way is to compare every card with every other card — that's a million comparisons. The fast way is to split the deck in half, sort each half, then merge them. Keep splitting halves until each pile is one card (already sorted!), then merge back up. That clever splitting is what the FFT does for Fourier transforms. Same answer, a thousand times less work.

A straight DFT on N samples does N² work — one sum of N terms for each of N output bins. The Fast Fourier Transform (Cooley & Tukey, 1965) computes the same answer in N · log2 N. For N = 1024 that's 10 240 operations instead of 1 048 576 — a 100× speedup. For a one-second audio clip at 48 kHz, minutes become milliseconds.

  1. Split by parity. Separate the input into even-indexed samples [x₀, x₂, x₄, …] and odd-indexed samples [x₁, x₃, x₅, …]. Each half has N/2 elements.
  2. Recurse. Run a DFT on each half. If a half is small enough, do it directly; otherwise split it again. Halving log2 N times gets you to size 1.
  3. Combine (butterfly). Each output bin k is Ek + WNk·Ok where E, O are the two sub-DFTs. The bin k + N/2 uses the same two numbers with just a sign flip — nearly free.

Intuition: doing one big DFT is like multiplying every sample by every frequency separately. Splitting by parity lets half the multiplications be shared between bins k and k + N/2, which recursively reduces redundant work at every level. The famous "butterfly" diagram is just a picture of those shared multiplications stacked log2 N layers deep.

Why blocks of 256 / 512 / 1024? Audio and image libraries pick powers of two because the split-in-half recursion only bottoms out cleanly when N is one. Non-power-of-two FFTs exist (Rader, Bluestein, mixed-radix) but they're slightly slower and trickier to implement.

Three more ways to think about the transform

The same DFT sits in different mental models depending on who's using it. A linear-algebra view explains what the transform is; the 2D extension explains how images plug in; and the DSP view explains why people bother.

1

Matrix + polar coordinates

The DFT is one matrix multiplication

Kid version: a matrix is just a grid of numbers. Imagine a multiplication table but filled with special spinning numbers. When you multiply your signal (a list of dots) by this grid, each row asks "how much of MY wiggle is inside your signal?" and the answer pops out. That's all a DFT is — one grid, one multiplication, all your frequencies.

Every frequency bin Xk is a dot product of the signal vector with one row of a fixed N×N matrix — the DFT matrix. Its entries are all powers of a single complex number ω = e−i 2π/N.

Each row of the matrix is a sampled complex exponential of a different frequency. Multiplying by the signal asks "how much of this exponential is in there?" The FFT is just a clever recursive way of doing this matrix multiply without writing the matrix out.

Each Xk is a complex number, which lives more naturally in polar form:

Kid version of "polar": you can give directions two ways.
XY way (Cartesian): "walk 3 blocks east and 4 blocks north".
Polar way: "walk 5 blocks facing northeast".
Same destination! Polar is (how far, which direction). A frequency ingredient in polar form is (how loud, where it starts) — or, on the canvas above, (how big a circle, starting angle).

The magnitude |Xk| is how much of that frequency; the phase φk is where it starts. That's literally what the epicycle canvas above is drawing — each circle's radius is |Xk|, its starting angle is φk, and its rotation speed is k cycles per trip around the path. The polar form is the picture.

2

Higher dimensions · 2D FFT

From 1D signals to 2D images

Kid version: a photo is a grid of pixels, so it's a 2D signal (it wiggles both across and down). The 2D FFT finds wiggles in both directions at once — stripes, checker patterns, big blobs. The trick: FFT every row first, then FFT every column. That's how JPEG photos get compressed — the computer breaks the image into 8×8 tiles and FFTs each tile.

An image is a 2D signal x[m, n]. The 2D DFT decomposes it into a grid of 2D "plane waves" — sinusoids that ripple in both directions at once.

The saving grace is separability: that 2D sum factors into a 1D FFT on every row, followed by a 1D FFT on every column. A naive 2D DFT of an N×N image would cost N⁴; the row-then-column FFT strategy brings it down to ~2·N²·log2 N.

Common block sizes: 8×8 — JPEG DCT blocks 16×16 — video motion estimation 32×32 & 64×64 — FFT-based image convolution, texture synthesis

Smaller blocks localise the spectrum (they only mix nearby pixels); larger blocks catch coarser patterns but smear local detail. JPEG's 8×8 is a compromise between compressibility and visible block artifacts; 32×32 or 64×64 give much better frequency resolution for scientific imaging.

★

Matrix factorization proof

Why N² becomes N log N — the Cooley–Tukey derivation

Kid version: the DFT grid has a hidden secret — the numbers inside aren't random, they're all powers of one "spinning" number. That's a lot of repetition! We can take the big grid and write it as a product of smaller grids, then multiply each smaller grid one after the other. Doing a few small multiplications in a row beats one giant one. The math below makes that "break-it-down" idea precise.

The "split by parity, recurse" story above is really a matrix factorization in disguise. Making it explicit shows why the FFT has to exist: the DFT matrix has so much algebraic redundancy that it can be written as a product of sparse pieces, and applying those pieces in sequence is dramatically cheaper than applying the dense matrix directly.

Step 1 · every DFT entry is a root of unity

Define ωN = ei 2π/N. These are the N-th roots of unity — N equally spaced points on the unit circle. For N = 6 it's a regular hexagon; for N = 32 it's a 32-gon; and so on. Every entry of the DFT matrix is one of these raised to some power.

The symmetry that unlocks the speedup is that ωNk + N/2 = −ωNk — half the powers are just sign flips of the other half. That's a lot of redundancy to exploit.

The permutation is free.

The same trick applies to each FN/2, which splits into two FN/4's, and so on. After log2 N levels the recursion reaches F1 = [1] and every layer between is just butterflies and permutations. The operation count satisfies the classic recurrence:

Step 4 · the payoff

For N = 1024 = 210:

  • Direct DFT: N² = 1,048,576 multiplications — over a million.
  • FFT: (N/2) · log2 N = 512 · 10 = 5,120 — just 5× N.
  • Speedup: ~200×.

The philosophical takeaway: FFT is a form of information compression — not of the data, but of the work needed to analyse it. The DFT matrix's algebraic structure (everything is a root-of-unity power) lets us factor a dense N² object into log2 N sparse layers. Same output; orders of magnitude less effort. That's why Gilbert Strang called the FFT "the most important algorithm of our lifetime" and why it shows up in every corner of numerical computing.

3

Digital signal processing

Where engineers actually use this

Kid version: DSP stands for Digital Signal Processing — using computer math on things like music, pictures, and radio waves. FFT is DSP's favourite tool. Think removing the bass from a song (filtering), seeing which pitches are in someone's voice (spectrogram), or squeezing a song into a tiny MP3 file (compression). Same math under the hood.

In DSP, the FFT is a tool, not a trophy. It earns its keep in three big ways:

  • Filtering via the convolution theorem. Convolving in time is the same as multiplying in frequency. To apply a low-pass filter to an audio signal: FFT the signal, multiply each bin by the filter's response, inverse FFT back. Goes from N² additions to N·log N — the move behind every real-time reverb, noise reduction, and echo cancelling plugin.
  • Spectral analysis. Watching |Xk| change over time (a spectrogram) reveals a signal's fingerprint: pitch in speech, modes in a vibrating bridge, a tumour's texture under ultrasound, interference in a radio link.
  • Lossy compression. MP3, JPEG, and MPEG all transform into a frequency-like domain, quantise small coefficients aggressively, and rely on the human eye/ear not missing what they threw away. Without the FFT's speed, those codecs wouldn't be feasible on consumer hardware.

That one-line identity — convolution in time, multiplication in frequency — is the reason the FFT shows up in places that look nothing like spinning circles: polynomial multiplication, large-integer arithmetic, cross-correlation, GPS signal acquisition, partial differential equation solvers.
