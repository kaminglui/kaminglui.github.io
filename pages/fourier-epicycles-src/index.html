Read this first

  1. What it does Any closed path is a sum of rotating vectors. The DFT splits your stroke into frequencies; the visualizer draws those vectors as nested circles that retrace your drawing.
  2. Try this Pick a preset or sketch a shape, then drag Precision down to 3–5. The path coarsens but you can see exactly which frequencies are doing the work.
  3. Look for this Large low-frequency circles set the overall shape; small high-frequency ones add detail. Open the math panel to check coefficient magnitude |Xk| and phase φk.

Why can stacked circles draw anything?

One rotating vector traces a circle. Attach a second vector to the tip of the first — spinning faster or slower — and the combined tip carves a richer curve: lobes, loops, even sharp cusps. Stack a third, a fourth, and every extra term adds finer detail.

The Fourier claim is that with enough of these stacked rotations, at the right speeds and starting phases, you can retrace any closed path exactly. The DFT is just the recipe that reads your drawing and tells you each circle's radius (|Xk|), starting angle (φk), and rotation speed (k cycles per trip around the path).

Beyond the visualizer

Four flavours of Fourier — and the FFT that makes them fast

FT FS DTFT DFT

The canvas above runs a DFT every frame. "Fourier transform" actually names a family of four related transforms, one for each way your signal can be continuous or discrete in time and one-shot or repeating.

The duality: whenever one domain is discrete, the other becomes periodic. Sample in time → periodic spectrum. Periodic in time → discrete spectrum. Four quadrants fall out of that rule.

Continuous · Aperiodic

Fourier Transform FT

The classic, "general" Fourier transform. Takes an arbitrary continuous signal and decomposes it into a continuous spectrum of frequencies.

Picture: a single clap in a quiet room — one-off, analogue, everything is smooth.

Continuous · Periodic

Fourier Series FS

A continuous signal that repeats every T seconds is built only from integer multiples of the fundamental frequency. The spectrum becomes a discrete ladder of harmonics.

Picture: a violin note held steady — periodic analogue waveform, harmonics at 1×, 2×, 3× … the fundamental.

Discrete · Aperiodic

Discrete-Time FT DTFT

The signal is sampled at integer times but never repeats. The output is continuous in frequency — and it's also periodic with period 2π (sampling folds frequencies back on themselves).

Picture: an endless stream of microphone samples you analyse as a theoretical object. You wouldn't compute it directly — it's infinite.

Discrete · Periodic

Discrete Fourier Transform DFT

The one a computer actually computes. Take N samples, assume they repeat, and get back N frequency bins. Finite in both domains — perfect for a CPU.

Picture: this visualizer! Every frame of the epicycle animation above comes straight from a DFT of your drawing.

Why the FFT is a big deal

A straight DFT on N samples does N² work — one sum of N terms for each of N output bins. The Fast Fourier Transform (Cooley & Tukey, 1965) computes the same answer in N · log2 N. For N = 1024 that's 10 240 operations instead of 1 048 576 — a 100× speedup. For a one-second audio clip at 48 kHz, minutes become milliseconds.

  1. Split by parity. Separate the input into even-indexed samples [x₀, x₂, x₄, …] and odd-indexed samples [x₁, x₃, x₅, …]. Each half has N/2 elements.
  2. Recurse. Run a DFT on each half. If a half is small enough, do it directly; otherwise split it again. Halving log2 N times gets you to size 1.
  3. Combine (butterfly). Each output bin k is Ek + WNk·Ok where E, O are the two sub-DFTs. The bin k + N/2 uses the same two numbers with just a sign flip — nearly free.

Intuition: doing one big DFT is like multiplying every sample by every frequency separately. Splitting by parity lets half the multiplications be shared between bins k and k + N/2, which recursively reduces redundant work at every level. The famous "butterfly" diagram is just a picture of those shared multiplications stacked log2 N layers deep.

Why blocks of 256 / 512 / 1024? Audio and image libraries pick powers of two because the split-in-half recursion only bottoms out cleanly when N is one. Non-power-of-two FFTs exist (Rader, Bluestein, mixed-radix) but they're slightly slower and trickier to implement.
