In plain English — what is optimisation?

Imagine a hilly landscape with a ball on it. Optimisation is the problem of "where's the lowest point?" — and gradient descent is the simplest recipe for answering it: feel the slope under your feet and take a small step downhill. Repeat.

Every modern Machine Learning model is a function fθ with millions of knobs θ. We pick a loss L(θ) measuring "how wrong is fθ?" on data. Training = using gradient descent on L to find θ that makes the loss small. Deep learning's big trick is that backprop computes ∇θ L cheaply even when fθ has billions of knobs.

Words that show up a lot:

  • Loss L(θ) — the number we're trying to minimise. Squared error, cross-entropy, KL divergence, reward-negated, ….
  • Gradient ∇L — a vector of partial derivatives. Points in the direction of steepest increase of L; we step the opposite way.
  • Learning rate α — how big a step to take. Too big: overshoot. Too small: slow.
  • Convex — a loss with a single bowl-shaped minimum. GD always finds it.
  • Non-convex — multiple valleys, saddle points, plateaus. GD can get stuck in a local min.
  • argmin / argmax — "the input that achieves the min/max". argmin L = θ*.
  • Eigenvalue / eigenvector — directions a matrix just scales (doesn't rotate). The axes PCA finds.

Math tour

1 · Reading min, max, argmin, argmax — and Lagrange

Kid version. min is "the lowest score a set ever reaches." argmin is "the input that achieves that lowest score." Same pair for max. When there's a rule you can't break (a constraint), Lagrange multipliers are the book- keeping trick that lets you still use plain gradient descent.

Notation:

  • — the lowest value of f. A number.
  • — the input x that achieves the lowest value. A point (or set of points if there are ties).
  • , — the same pair for maximising.

Critical fact: argmin is not min. If f(x) = (x − 3)², then min = 0 but argmin = 3. Machine Learning people say "minimise L" but almost always want the parameters that achieve the minimum, i.e. argmin.

Supremum / infimum. sup and inf are the "just-in-case" versions of max and min. If a set doesn't actually contain its largest element — for instance, the open interval (0, 1) has no maximum — then sup exists anyway as the least upper bound (sup(0,1) = 1). min / max live in the set; sup / inf live in its closure.

Constrained optimisation. "Minimise f(x) subject to g(x) = 0." The Lagrangian turns a constrained problem into an unconstrained one:

  • λ — Lagrange multiplier: a scalar you also solve for. Roughly, "how much does the constraint hurt?"

2 · Gradient descent and SGD

Kid version. Stand on a hill with your eyes closed. Feel which way is downhill with your feet. Step that way. Repeat. That's gradient descent.

Given a differentiable loss L(θ), gradient descent iterates:

  • α — learning rate; the step-size scalar. Too big: overshoot or diverge. Too small: crawl.
  • ∇L — gradient vector; each entry is ∂L/∂θi. Points uphill; we step opposite.

On a convex loss (a "bowl"), GD provably finds the global minimum with a small-enough α. Every classical method before neural nets (linear regression, logistic regression, linear SVM, lasso) has a convex loss — so "just run GD" was the full story.

SGD — stochastic gradient descent — replaces the full gradient with a noisy estimate from a single sample or mini-batch:

Cheap (doesn't touch the whole dataset) and a blessing in non-convex land (the noise helps escape saddle points and narrow ridges). GD + momentum keeps a running average of gradients — a "heavy ball" that powers through flat regions:

Adam, RMSprop, AdaGrad, AdamW all build on this skeleton with per-parameter scaling.

3 · Non-convex optimisation and escape tricks

Kid version. Deep-learning landscapes aren't nice bowls. They have many valleys (local minima), saddles (looks flat in one direction, cliff in another), and plateaus. Pure downhill-stepping can get stuck. The trick: add randomness that lets you occasionally go up.

Common escape methods:

Momentum / Adam

  • Average gradients over time.
  • Flat plateaus get crossed faster.
  • Doesn't escape a deep local min — only accelerates.

SGD noise

  • Stochastic gradient acts like injected noise.
  • Perturbs you off saddles, over shallow ridges.
  • Main escape mechanism in modern deep learning.

Simulated annealing

  • Propose a random step. Always accept improvements.
  • Accept worsening steps with probability decreasing in ΔL and in 1/T.
  • Cool T slowly. Classical for combinatorial optimisation (TSP, graph colouring).

Restarts / ensembles

  • Run GD from many random inits; pick the best.
  • Embarrassingly parallel; baseline for "just try harder."
  • Used in hyper-parameter search and population-based training.

For modern deep nets, plain SGD with momentum + a schedule is the workhorse. The curious empirical fact: in the high-dimensional parameter spaces of deep nets, most critical points are saddles, not minima, and the SGD noise handles them — simulated annealing is rarely used in deep learning, but it's still the go-to for discrete/combinatorial problems where gradients don't exist.

4 · Linear algebra — transformations, dot / cross, convolution

Kid version. A matrix is a rule for turning one arrow into another — stretch, rotate, shear. The dot product measures "how much two arrows point the same way." Convolution is dot-products slid across an image to make a new one.

A matrix A ∈ ℝm×n is a linear transformation that maps vectors in ℝn to ℝm. Three facts to internalise:

  • Columns are images of basis vectors. Column j of A is what A does to the j-th unit vector ej. A transformation is fully specified by what it does to the basis.
  • Matrix-vector product is a linear combination of A's columns: .
  • Composition = multiplication. Doing A then B equals applying BA as one matrix. Order matters: BA ≠ AB in general.

Dot product — the most-used operation in Machine Learning:

  • Measures alignment. Positive: same-ish direction. Zero: orthogonal. Negative: opposite.
  • Attention scores in transformers are dot products. "Similarity" in retrieval is dot product. Logistic regression's output is w · x + b.

Cross product (ℝ³ only) — produces a vector orthogonal to both inputs:

  • Magnitude = area of the parallelogram spanned by a, b.
  • Used in physics (torque, angular momentum) and 3D graphics (surface normals). Rare in Machine Learning.

Convolution — a sliding dot product:

  • Translation-equivariant: shift the input, shifted output.
  • In CNNs, each filter is a small k weighted-summed across image patches — a dot product per pixel.
  • Convolution theorem: . Fourier turns convolution into pointwise multiplication.

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors. A vector v is an eigenvector of A if : the transformation leaves v's direction alone, only scaling it by λ (the eigenvalue). These are the "natural axes" of A. Symmetric A always has a full orthogonal eigenbasis — the fact PCA leans on in §5.

5 · PCA — find the direction of maximum variance

Kid version. You have a cloud of points. PCA asks: "If I had to squash you all onto a single line, which line would lose the least information?" Answer: the direction the cloud is most stretched along.

Given N points xi ∈ ℝd with mean x̄, centre them and form the covariance:

Σ is symmetric positive semi-definite, so it has an eigendecomposition with orthonormal eigenvectors u1, …, ud and non-negative eigenvalues λ1 ≥ … ≥ λd ≥ 0. The principal components are the ui; the explained variance of the i-th is λi / Σj λj.

Three equivalent ways to derive PCA, all giving the same answer:

  • Maximum variance. u1 = argmax‖u‖=1 u⊤ Σ u. A constrained optimisation; solve it with Lagrange multipliers and you get Σu = λu.
  • Minimum reconstruction error. u1 minimises Σi ‖xi − (u u⊤) xi‖². Same solution.
  • SVD of centered data. Let X̃ be the N×d matrix of centered points. SVD X̃ = UΣV⊤. Columns of V are the principal components; squared singular values / N are the eigenvalues of Σ.

Uses: dimensionality reduction (project on top-k PCs), denoising (drop the low-variance components), whitening (normalise by eigenvalues), visualisation (project high-dim data into 2D). Not a clustering method — if you want that, go back to K-means or GMM.

6 · Neural networks as universal approximators

Kid version. A single number times a weight plus a bias is just a line. A pile of those, with a non-linear bend in the middle, can draw any curve you want. That's the whole reason neural nets are used everywhere: they can copy almost any function you hand them.

A single layer computes . Three pieces:

  • Wx — linear transformation (§4). Stretches, rotates, projects.
  • + b — bias. Translates.
  • σ(·) — non-linear activation applied elementwise: ReLU, sigmoid, GELU, tanh. Without it, stacked layers collapse to one big linear transform.

The Universal Approximation Theorem (Cybenko 1989, Hornik 1991): for any continuous function f on a compact domain and any ε > 0, there exists a two-layer neural network N(x) = W2 σ(W1 x + b1) + b2 with finite width such that

In words: neural networks with a non-polynomial activation can approximate any continuous function to arbitrary precision — if they're wide enough.

Important caveats the pop-sci version skips: (1) the theorem doesn't say how wide — the width can be exponential in the input dimension. (2) It doesn't say you can train the weights to match — only that a setting exists. (3) Modern practice relies on depth (many layers) vastly more than width; deep networks represent some functions with exponentially fewer parameters than wide ones. Universal approximation is why NNs could learn almost anything; SGD, architectures, and data are why they actually do.

This is why a neural network can be the policy πθ(a|s) in Reinforcement Learning, the score network sθ(x, t) in diffusion, the encoder/decoder of a VAE, the value function Vφ, or the classifier in a supervised model — it's the one function class flexible enough to parameterise any of those.

7 · Probability & distributions

Kid version. A distribution is a rule that says how likely each outcome is. A coin flip has two outcomes. A die roll has six. The height of a random adult has a whole continuum. Most of Machine Learning boils down to picking a distribution that could have generated your data, then turning the knobs until it matches.

A random variable X takes values in some set — the integers, the reals, ℝd. Its distribution tells you the probability of each value (discrete case, PMF) or the density of values (continuous case, PDF). Every PDF integrates to 1; every PMF sums to 1.

The usual cast

Six distributions show up everywhere. Learn the shapes and you can read half of Machine Learning papers on sight.

They're mostly the same distribution

Most of these aren't separate objects — they're different corners of one family tree. Knowing the relationships lets you translate between them without memorising anything new.

Two theorems that do most of the work

Law of Large Numbers (LLN). If X1, X2, … are i.i.d. with mean μ, the sample mean converges to μ:

This is why Monte Carlo works. It's why averaging many noisy gradient samples in SGD gives you something that looks like the true gradient.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT). Rescale that sample mean by √n and the fluctuations around μ have a Gaussian shape, whatever the source distribution was (as long as its variance is finite):

Heavy-tailed caveat. CLT needs finite variance. The Cauchy distribution violates this — its sample mean stays just as noisy as a single sample, forever. This matters in Reinforcement Learning (reward outliers), SGD (gradient noise in some architectures), and finance (why the 2008 "6σ event" wasn't actually 6σ — the real tails were fatter than the Gaussian model assumed).

Gamma — sums of exponentials, CLT in one picture

Kid version. One Poisson event arrives after a random exponential wait. Want to know how long until the k-th event? Add together k independent exponential waits. That sum has a name: Gamma(k, θ). As k grows the sum's distribution gets more and more bell-shaped — a direct, visible consequence of the Central Limit Theorem.

Formally, if X1, …, Xk are i.i.d. Exp(θ), then their sum is Gamma(k, θ):

Two corners you already know live in this family: Gamma(1, θ) = Exp(θ), and Gamma(k, θ) converges to 𝒩(kθ, kθ²) for large k. Concrete example — an ad banner gets clicks at rate λ = 0.5 per second (so θ = 1/λ = 2 seconds between clicks). The wait until the third click is Gamma(3, 2), with expected value kθ = 6 seconds.

Conjugate priors — the Bayesian coin flip

Kid version. A prior is what you believed before looking at the data; a posterior is the updated belief. If your prior and your observation model are conjugate, the posterior lives in the same family as the prior and updating reduces to arithmetic on the parameters — no integrals required.

For a coin with unknown bias θ ∈ (0, 1), the Beta(α, β) distribution is the conjugate prior to the Bernoulli likelihood. Observe k heads in n flips and the posterior is another Beta:

(α, β) act as pseudocounts: α ≈ "prior heads", β ≈ "prior tails". A Beta(1, 1) prior is uniform — maximally uninformative — so after k heads in n flips the posterior mean is Laplace's rule of succession. Pick a prior, add observations, and watch the posterior sharpen:

Multivariate — the Dirichlet on a 3-sided die

Kid version. A coin has two sides; a die has many. If the unknown thing is a set of probabilities (pA, pB, pC) that sum to 1, the natural conjugate prior is the Dirichlet — the multivariate Beta. Observe a bunch of rolls, tally counts, add them to the α vector, and you have the posterior. Same pseudocount arithmetic; one more dimension.

For K outcomes, the Dirichlet is a density on the (K−1)-simplex with parameter vector α = (α1, …, αK):

Geometric — waiting for the first success

Kid version. You flip a biased coin over and over until it finally comes up heads. How many flips did that take? That random count is Geometric. "Rare event that eventually happens" is its whole personality: how many ad impressions until a click, how many network packets until one drops, how many test runs until the first bug.

If each trial succeeds independently with probability p, the number of trials up to and including the first success is Geometric(p):

Mean 1/p, variance (1−p)/p². Geometric is the discrete sibling of the Exponential — both are memoryless: after 5 failures, the remaining wait has the exact same distribution as starting over. They are the only distributions on their supports with that property, which is why the memoryless assumption pins them down uniquely in queuing theory, reliability analysis, and Markov chains.

Toy example — bug in a web form. Every form submit has a p = 0.3 chance of triggering a latent validation bug. How many submits until the first trigger? E[X] = 1/p ≈ 3.33 submits. Probability the bug shows up on exactly the 5th submit: P(X = 5) = (1−p)4·p = 0.7⁴·0.3 ≈ 0.072. Probability you survive the first three submits without triggering anything: P(X > 3) = 0.7³ = 0.343. And by memorylessness, once you've survived three submits, the remaining wait still has the same Geometric(0.3) distribution — history doesn't help you predict when the bug will finally fire.

PMF · PDF · CDF — three views of the same distribution

PMF (discrete) and PDF (continuous) answer "how much mass at this point?". The Cumulative Distribution Function answers the related question "how much mass at or below this point?":

A CDF is always monotone non-decreasing, starts at 0, and ends at 1. For discrete variables it's a staircase — flat between atoms, jumping by the PMF value at each one. For continuous variables it's smooth, and its derivative is the PDF. Three reasons it's worth knowing as a separate object from the density:

  • Interval probabilities are one subtraction: .
  • The CDF is what you invert to sample. If U ∼ Uniform(0, 1) then F−1(U) has distribution F — the basis of every non-Gaussian random-number generator in every library.
  • The empirical CDF is the plot behind quantile–quantile checks, Kolmogorov–Smirnov goodness-of-fit tests, and scipy's percentileofscore.

Toy example — fair six-sided die. PMF: p(k) = 1/6 for k = 1…6. CDF: F(1) = 1/6, F(2) = 2/6, …, F(6) = 6/6 = 1 — a staircase with six equal steps. P(roll ≤ 3) = F(3) = 1/2. P(2 ≤ roll ≤ 5) = F(5) − F(1) = 5/6 − 1/6 = 4/6 ≈ 0.667. And if U ∼ Uniform(0, 1) the inverse-CDF rule gives X = ⌈6U⌉ — drop your uniform-sample into the staircase and read off which step catches you. That one line is how every library samples from arbitrary discrete distributions.

Histogram — the empirical version of PMF / PDF

When the distribution is unknown but you have samples x1, …, xN, a histogram is the empirical stand-in. Split the range into bins of width h, count the samples in each bin, divide by N·h, and you have a piecewise-constant estimator of p(x):

Bin width h is the one knob, and it trades bias against variance. Too narrow: each bin has few samples, so the estimate is noisy (high variance). Too wide: sharp features of p get smoothed into a single bar (high bias). Scott's rule (h ≈ 3.5 σ N−1/3) and Freedman–Diaconis (h ≈ 2·IQR·N−1/3) are common defaults that trade the two automatically.

Where histograms show up. Digital image processing — pixel-intensity histograms drive auto-exposure, Otsu thresholding, and histogram equalisation (remap the CDF to uniform, pull out detail). DSP — power-per-frequency-bin histograms are the spectrogram. Machine learning — class-frequency histograms diagnose imbalance, and the discretisation inside decision-tree splits is just an argmax over a histogram of label entropies.

Toy example — 100 uniform samples, 10 bins. Draw 100 values from Uniform(0, 10) and bucket them into ten bins of width h = 1. The true density is p(x) = 1/10 = 0.1. One realisation of the counts might be [8, 11, 9, 12, 10, 9, 11, 10, 12, 8]; dividing by N·h = 100 gives [0.08, 0.11, 0.09, 0.12, 0.10, 0.09, 0.11, 0.10, 0.12, 0.08] — all within a few percent of the true 0.1. Shrink h to 0.1 (100 bins): each bin now has ~1 sample, the estimator becomes spiky and useless. Grow h to 5 (2 bins): each bin has ~50 samples, perfectly flat, but you can no longer tell a uniform from a gentle slope. That's the bias-variance knob.

Importance sampling — when rare events dominate the answer

Kid version. You want the average damage a software bug causes. The bug shows up once in 10,000 runs and is catastrophic when it does. Randomly sampling 1,000 runs gives you ~1,000 zeros and probably no bug at all — a useless estimate. The fix: deliberately run tests in a bug-prone mode that surfaces bugs 1 time in 10, collect real damage numbers, and reweight the average to correct for the fact that you biased the sampling.

The plain Monte Carlo estimator of Ep[f(X)] averages f(xi) over samples from p. If f(x) is zero except in a rare region of p, nearly every draw contributes nothing. Importance sampling substitutes a proposal distribution q that oversamples the rare-but-important region, then reweights each evaluation by the density ratio p/q to keep the expectation unbiased:

The estimator is unbiased as long as q(x) > 0 everywhere p(x)·f(x) ≠ 0. The art is picking q: if q tracks the shape of |f·p| well, variance drops by orders of magnitude; if q misses important support of f, a few huge weights blow up the variance and the estimate becomes useless. "Effective sample size" (ESS) = (Σwi)² / Σwi² is the standard diagnostic — a 10,000-sample importance estimate with ESS = 12 is secretly a 12-sample estimate.

Three places you'll meet it
  • Software / hardware reliability testing. A server crashes 1 time in 10⁶ requests. Replaying production traffic (p) for 10⁵ tests sees no crashes and tells you nothing about the damage distribution. Instead, inject a fault-biased workload q that crashes 1 in 10², measure per-crash cost, and multiply each measured cost by the ratio p/q ≈ 10⁻⁶ / 10⁻² = 10⁻⁴ when averaging. You recover the production-scale expectation using ~10⁴× fewer runs. This is the core of rare-event Monte Carlo in queueing, network reliability, insurance, and nuclear safety.
  • Reinforcement learning — off-policy + trust region. TRPO, PPO, Retrace, IMPALA, and every modern actor-critic need to estimate expected return under a new policy πnew using trajectories sampled from an old policy πold. The weight πnew(a|s) / πold(a|s) is a direct importance-sampling ratio. TRPO bounds the KL divergence between old and new to keep the ratio well-behaved; PPO clips the ratio to [1−ε, 1+ε] — exactly the "q mismatched to p blows up variance" failure mode showing up as an engineering constraint.
  • Supervised learning under covariate shift. Training data drawn from q(x), deployment from p(x) — the "deployment doesn't look like training" problem. Empirical risk on q gives the best fit to q, not p. If you can estimate the density ratio p(xi) / q(xi) (directly, or via a domain classifier), reweight each training example by it and the reweighted loss is an unbiased estimator of the deployment risk. Same mechanism underlies class-imbalance correction (reweight rare classes up) and propensity-score adjustment in causal inference.

Every Machine Learning loss has a distribution story under it. Squared error ↔ Gaussian MLE. Cross-entropy ↔ categorical MLE. Binary cross-entropy ↔ Bernoulli MLE. Pick a distribution for your noise, write down its log-likelihood, and you've derived the loss — see Machine Learning · §2 for the full walk.

8 · Information theory — entropy, KL, cross-entropy

Kid version. Entropy is how surprised you are, on average, by what the next symbol will be. A fair coin is maximally surprising. A two-headed coin is zero surprise. KL divergence is how much extra surprise you suffer when you use the wrong guess about the coin. Cross-entropy loss is entropy + KL, and it's the reason classification models use the loss they use.

Entropy — average surprise

Suppose you pick a symbol from a discrete distribution p. The surprise of seeing outcome x is — small if p(x) is large, huge if p(x) is tiny. Entropy H(p) is the average surprise over the distribution:

Units: nats if log is natural, bits if log₂. Two properties you want to build intuition for: H ≥ 0 always; H is maximised by the uniform distribution (if you have no information, every outcome is equally surprising).

Toy examples. Fair coin (p = 0.5): H = −2·0.5·log₂(0.5) = 1 bit. Biased coin (p = 0.9): H = −0.9·log₂(0.9) − 0.1·log₂(0.1) ≈ 0.469 bit — the 0.9 outcome is mostly expected, so most bits you "spend" on the message are wasted on what you already knew. Fair 6-sided die: H = log₂ 6 ≈ 2.585 bit. Two-headed coin: H = 0 bits — no uncertainty, no information. That's the reason entropy is also called "information content".

KL divergence — cost of the wrong guess

Toy example — two coins. Reality is a fair coin: p(H) = p(T) = 0.5. You've been told the coin is heavily biased: q(H) = 0.9, q(T) = 0.1. Plug in and compute:

Two takeaways. Both are positive — every misspecified guess costs you. They're different numbers — KL is not symmetric. The asymmetry has a story: if you use q to code samples from p, the 0.5 mass at T lands in a near-empty bin under q and pays log(5) ≈ 1.6 nat of surprise every time it shows up. The reverse direction doesn't punish mismatched tails as hard, so the two numbers land in different places.

Properties: KL ≥ 0, with equality iff p = q (Gibbs' inequality); KL is not symmetric — DKL(p‖q) ≠ DKL(q‖p). The asymmetry matters: KL(p‖q) penalises q for assigning low mass where p has high mass ("mode-covering"), while KL(q‖p) penalises q for spreading mass where p has none ("mode-seeking"). Variational inference and GAN objectives pick different asymmetries for exactly this reason.

Cross-entropy — entropy + KL

Cross-entropy H(p, q) is the average surprise when you code for q but reality is p:

Reading the decomposition: H(p) is the irreducible part — you'd need that many bits even with the perfect model. KL(p‖q) is everything else, which is what you minimise when you train a classifier. Since H(p) doesn't depend on q, minimising cross-entropy and minimising KL are the same problem.

Toy example — a classifier. Truth says "cat", so the one-hot label is p = (1, 0, 0) over classes (cat, dog, bird). Your model predicts q = (0.7, 0.2, 0.1). The per-example cross-entropy is H(p, q) = −1·ln(0.7) − 0·ln(0.2) − 0·ln(0.1) ≈ 0.357 nat (≈ 0.515 bit). If q were perfectly confident (0.99, 0.005, 0.005), the loss drops to ≈ 0.010 nat; if q were completely wrong (0.1, 0.7, 0.2), it jumps to ≈ 2.303 nat. That's the gradient signal backprop turns into weight updates — every time the true class has low predicted probability, the loss spikes and pushes the logit up.

Mutual information — how much X tells you about Y

Mutual information measures the reduction in uncertainty about Y once you know X:

I(X; Y) = 0 iff X and Y are independent. The KL form says it directly: mutual information is how far the joint p(x, y) is from being the product of marginals. It's the information-theoretic lens on correlation, the supervision signal in contrastive learning (InfoNCE), and the quantity the "information bottleneck" view of deep learning tries to manage.

Toy examples. Two independent fair coins: p(x, y) = 0.25 for all four outcomes, and p(x)p(y) = 0.25 too — their ratio is 1, so I(X; Y) = 0 bit. Two coins that always match (X = Y): p(HH) = p(TT) = 0.5, p(HT) = p(TH) = 0, so knowing X pins down Y exactly and I(X; Y) = H(X) = 1 bit. A weather sensor with 10% miscalibration: P(reads rain | rain) = 0.9, P(reads rain | sun) = 0.1, equal priors → I ≈ 0.531 bit out of a possible 1, which is the fraction of the truth the sensor actually tells you. Training a contrastive model is literally pushing this number up.

Where this shows up. Squared error → Gaussian negative log-likelihood → cross-entropy with a Gaussian q. Softmax cross-entropy in classifiers → Machine Learning · §2. ELBO in VAE and diffusion = reconstruction − KL → Diffusion · §2. Entropy-regularised policies in Reinforcement Learning trade reward for H(π) to keep exploration alive → Reinforcement Learning Lab. KL always the same quantity; different signs and places.

9 · Fitting under KL — two failure modes

Kid version. Suppose the real world is bimodal — two bumps. You want to describe it with a single Gaussian. You can either cover both bumps with a wide blanket (moment-match / forward KL), or pick one bump and sit on it (reverse KL). Neither is "wrong" — they're minimising different losses. The whole point is that the answer depends on which direction of KL you wrote down.
