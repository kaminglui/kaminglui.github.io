Timeline & lineage

A history of the methods this site teaches

Every method was invented to solve a specific problem. Reading how ideas flowed from one to the next — what Shannon's entropy led to, why Bayes' theorem now powers Thompson sampling, how gradient descent found its way into transformers — makes it easier to remember which method to reach for and anticipate where the field goes next.

≈ 40 methods · 5 eras · ~260 years

Motivation, lineage, cross-links

Each card names the method, its year and its primary attribution, the problem it was invented to solve, and a one-line note on what it then influenced. Click the chevron link to jump to that method's home lab. Citing "the inventor" of an idea is often contested — I credit the most widely cited primary source and flag multiple authors where it matters.

How to read this. Each coloured bar on the left of a card marks its family. The Influenced line threads the DAG — follow the links to see what each idea seeded in the decades that came after.

Era 1 — Foundations

before 1950

Before anyone said "machine learning", mathematicians built the inference, optimisation, and signal-processing tools modern ML would later plug in. Every method below pre-dates the computer but is still running inside every deep-learning framework today.

  1. 1763

    Bayes' theorem

    Rev. Thomas Bayes (posthumous); edited and published by Richard Price.

    Motivation: reason backwards from effects to causes — what's the probability the coin is biased, given this observed sequence?

    Influenced: MLE (1912), Thompson sampling (1933), variational inference, every Bayesian model; the conjugate-prior machinery in Math §8 and the RL §1 coin demo.

  2. 1805

    Least squares

    Adrien-Marie Legendre (1805); Carl Friedrich Gauss (1809) with probabilistic justification.

    Motivation: fit a line to noisy astronomical observations — predict where Ceres would re-emerge from behind the Sun.

    Influenced: linear regression, PCA, MLE-for-Gaussian-noise (the squared-error loss in every deep network). See Machine Learning §1.

  3. 1807

    Fourier series

    Joseph Fourier, memoir on heat propagation (published 1822 as Théorie analytique de la chaleur).

    Motivation: solve the heat equation in a metal rod by writing temperature as a sum of sinusoids.

    Influenced: signal processing, the DFT/FFT, transformer positional encoding, the entire Fourier lab.

  4. 1810

    Central Limit Theorem

    Pierre-Simon Laplace (1810); special case by Abraham de Moivre (1733).

    Motivation: explain why sums of many independent noisy measurements end up Gaussian even when each piece isn't.

    Influenced: Gaussian MLE, large-sample statistics, the "noise is Gaussian" assumption in every regression loss. Proof-in-pictures in Math §7.

  5. 1847

    Gradient descent

    Augustin-Louis Cauchy, "Méthode générale pour la résolution des systèmes d'équations simultanées".

    Motivation: solve systems of equations iteratively by following the steepest local decrease — originally for fitting orbit computations.

    Influenced: stochastic GD, backpropagation, Adam, every optimiser in deep learning. The canonical demo lives in Math §2.

  6. 1901

    Principal component analysis

    Karl Pearson (1901); Harold Hotelling's statistical formalisation (1933).

    Motivation: find the line (then plane, then subspace) that best fits a cloud of points — minimum-total-squared-perpendicular-distance.

    Influenced: factor analysis, autoencoders, word embeddings; the spectral intuition behind eigendecomposition and Math §5 PCA.

  7. 1908

    Langevin dynamics

    Paul Langevin, equation for Brownian motion under a systematic force plus random kicks.

    Motivation: model a particle jiggling in a fluid — deterministic drift plus Gaussian noise.

    Influenced: SGD-as-Langevin, MCMC samplers, score-based diffusion sampling (Diffusion §5). The same random walk that powers forward diffusion.

  8. 1912

    Maximum likelihood estimation

    R. A. Fisher; formalised in a series of papers between 1912–1922.

    Motivation: a principled way to pick a parameter: the value that makes the observed data most probable.

    Influenced: logistic regression, softmax cross-entropy, the whole supervised-learning recipe. See Machine Learning §2 MLE derivation.

  9. 1933

    Thompson sampling

    William R. Thompson, "On the likelihood that one unknown probability exceeds another…"

    Motivation: design clinical trials — allocate patients between two treatments to balance learning which is better against curing the patient in front of you.

    Influenced: multi-armed bandits, Bayesian RL, modern A/B testing, RLHF data collection. Live demo in RL §1.

  10. 1948

    Shannon entropy & information theory

    Claude Shannon, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication".

    Motivation: quantify how much information a message carries — the fewest bits on average needed to transmit it.

    Influenced: KL divergence (below), cross-entropy loss, mutual information, the whole "loss function is a log-likelihood" worldview. Math §8 Information theory.

  11. 1951

    Kullback–Leibler divergence

    Solomon Kullback & Richard Leibler, "On Information and Sufficiency".

    Motivation: measure how far one probability distribution is from another, in a way that respects Shannon's coding-cost intuition.

    Influenced: variational inference, the ELBO, GAN objectives, diffusion losses. The asymmetry is why VI and MLE produce different fits — see Math §9 mode-covering vs mode-seeking.

Era 2 — AI foundations

1950 – 1990

Computers arrived. The first generation of learning machines tried to mimic neurons (perceptron), plan under uncertainty (dynamic programming), and approximate intractable integrals (EM). Most of the conceptual primitives for modern ML were in place by 1990 — we just didn't have the data or the compute yet.

  1. 1957

    Dynamic programming & the Bellman equation

    Richard Bellman, Dynamic Programming.

    Motivation: solve sequential decision problems (rocket guidance, inventory control) by recursing on a value function.

    Influenced: MDPs, TD learning, Q-learning, policy iteration, RL writ large. The recursion in RL §4 DP.

  2. 1958

    Perceptron

    Frank Rosenblatt, "The Perceptron: A Probabilistic Model for Information Storage…".

    Motivation: a brain-inspired machine that learns a linear classifier from examples, with weights adjusted by a simple rule.

    Influenced: multi-layer networks, the AI-winter debate (Minsky & Papert 1969 on XOR), backpropagation — everything that calls itself "deep learning".

  3. 1965

    Fast Fourier transform

    James Cooley & John Tukey; Gauss worked it out unpublished around 1805.

    Motivation: detect Soviet nuclear tests from seismic signals; compute the DFT in O(N log N) instead of O(N²).

    Influenced: every signal-processing and audio pipeline; spectrograms for Whisper and other audio transformers; the Fourier lab's entire architecture.

  4. 1977

    EM algorithm

    Dempster, Laird & Rubin, "Maximum Likelihood from Incomplete Data via the EM Algorithm".

    Motivation: do MLE when some variables are unobserved — alternate between guessing the missing values and updating parameters.

    Influenced: Gaussian mixture fitting, hidden Markov models, variational inference, the VAE E-step intuition. See Machine Learning §5 clustering.

  5. 1986

    Backpropagation

    Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams (Nature); earlier in Werbos' 1974 thesis.

    Motivation: efficiently compute gradients through many-layer networks by reusing the chain rule; unlocked training hidden-layer models.

    Influenced: CNNs, RNNs, LSTMs, transformers, every deep-learning library's autograd. Universal approximation (1989) explains why it matters — see Math §6.

  6. 1988

    Temporal-difference learning

    Richard Sutton, "Learning to predict by the methods of temporal differences".

    Motivation: learn value estimates by bootstrapping — updating from the next state's estimate rather than waiting for the final return.

    Influenced: Q-learning, SARSA, deep RL (DQN), actor-critic TD targets. The "same-shape update" story in RL §5.

  7. 1989

    Q-learning

    Chris Watkins (thesis); formal convergence proof with Peter Dayan (1992).

    Motivation: learn the optimal action-value function directly from experience, without needing a model of the environment.

    Influenced: DQN (2013), double DQN, Rainbow, AlphaGo's value head. The model-free anchor in RL §5.

  8. 1989

    Universal approximation theorem

    George Cybenko; Hornik, Stinchcombe & White (1989–1991).

    Motivation: prove that a two-layer neural network with a non-polynomial activation can approximate any continuous function on a compact set.

    Influenced: the theoretical licence to use NNs as policies, value functions, score networks — anywhere a flexible function class is needed. See Math §6.

Era 3 — Modern ML

1990 – 2010

Kernel methods, convex optimisation, and probabilistic graphical models dominated. Neural nets survived on the margins — LSTM, gradient tricks, score matching — waiting for compute and data to catch up.

  1. 1992

    REINFORCE

    Ronald Williams, "Simple statistical gradient-following algorithms for connectionist reinforcement learning".

    Motivation: optimise a parameterised policy by ascending the gradient of expected reward — directly, without a value function.

    Influenced: actor-critic, TRPO, PPO, SAC, RLHF fine-tuning. The policy-gradient identity in RL §7.

  2. 1995

    Support vector machine

    Vladimir Vapnik & co. — margin formulation in Boser, Guyon & Vapnik (1992); popularised by Vapnik (1995).

    Motivation: build the widest-margin separator — maximise the distance from the decision boundary to the nearest training point.

    Influenced: the canonical kernel method — dominated tabular / text ML from 1995 to ~2012 before deep learning overtook it on perception tasks. The "regularisation = margin" intuition recurs in deep learning (large-margin softmax, contrastive losses). Machine Learning §4 SVM demo.

  3. 1997

    LSTM

    Sepp Hochreiter & Jürgen Schmidhuber, "Long Short-Term Memory".

    Motivation: fix the vanishing-gradient problem in RNNs by adding gated cells that preserve information across long time spans.

    Influenced: the canonical gated-RNN. GRU (Cho et al. 2014) is the simpler two-gate cousin. Together they powered seq2seq, neural machine translation, and speech recognition — the state of the art until transformers superseded them around 2018.

  4. 2002

    UCB1

    Auer, Cesa-Bianchi & Fischer, "Finite-time analysis of the multi-armed bandit problem".

    Motivation: "optimism in the face of uncertainty" — pick the arm with the highest upper confidence bound, balancing exploration and exploitation with a provable log-regret bound.

    Influenced: MCTS (UCT), Bayesian optimisation, contextual bandits. See the strategy selector in the RL Lab bandit demo.

  5. 2005

    Score matching

    Aapo Hyvärinen, "Estimation of Non-Normalized Statistical Models by Score Matching".

    Motivation: fit a density model without computing the intractable normalisation constant — match the score ∇ log p instead.

    Influenced: denoising score matching, score-based generative models, the score network in DDPM. The math behind Diffusion §5.

  6. 2006

    Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT)

    Rémi Coulom; Kocsis & Szepesvári formalised UCT the same year.

    Motivation: plan in large game trees where you have a simulator but no value function — UCB1 applied recursively down the search tree.

    Influenced: AlphaGo, AlphaZero, MuZero; LLM-guided theorem provers. Full coverage in RL §8.

  7. 2009

    ImageNet

    Jia Deng, Wei Dong, Richard Socher, Li-Jia Li, Kai Li & Fei-Fei Li (Stanford), CVPR 2009.

    Motivation: a labelled image dataset big enough to regularise genuinely deep networks — 14M images across 21k WordNet synsets, with the 1000-class ILSVRC subset (~1.3M training images) as the annual benchmark starting 2010.

    Influenced: AlexNet (2012) won ILSVRC and kicked off the deep-learning era; every CNN and ViT since was benchmarked here. One of the three preconditions — dataset, GPU, ReLU — that converged in 2012.

Era 4 — Deep-learning wave

2010 – 2017

Three preconditions converged around 2012 and ignited modern deep learning: NVIDIA GPUs (CUDA shipped in 2006) gave neural nets a 10–30× speedup over CPUs; ImageNet (2009) provided 1.3M labelled training images across 1000 classes — enough to regularise genuinely deep networks without overfitting; and ReLU (Nair & Hinton, 2010; Glorot, Bordes & Bengio, 2011) replaced saturating sigmoid/tanh activations, fixing the vanishing-gradient behaviour that had crippled deep stacks since the 1990s. AlexNet stitched all three together in 2012 and cut ImageNet top-5 error from 26% to 15% overnight. Old neural-network ideas stopped being curiosities — representation learning, generative modelling, and attention emerged in that order, each one unblocking the next.

  1. 2010

    ReLU activation

    Vinod Nair & Geoffrey Hinton, ICML 2010 (for RBMs); Xavier Glorot, Antoine Bordes & Yoshua Bengio, AISTATS 2011, "Deep Sparse Rectifier Neural Networks" (for deep feed-forward nets). Earlier: Fukushima's Neocognitron (1980) used the positive-part function.

    Motivation: replace sigmoid / tanh — both saturate at the extremes and kill gradients — with max(0, x), whose derivative is 1 for positive inputs and 0 for negative. Faster to compute, no vanishing-gradient compression at the ends.

    Influenced: the activation that made genuinely deep networks trainable. AlexNet credited ReLU for most of its training-time speed-up; variants (Leaky ReLU, ELU, GELU for transformers, SiLU for Llama) dominate every deep-learning library.

  2. 2012

    AlexNet

    Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever & Geoffrey Hinton (University of Toronto), "ImageNet Classification with Deep Convolutional Neural Networks", NeurIPS 2012. Immediate GPU-CNN predecessor: Ciresan et al., IJCNN 2011.

    Motivation: stitch the three preconditions together — the ImageNet dataset (2009), NVIDIA GPUs with CUDA (two GTX 580s for 5–6 days of training), and the ReLU activation (2010) — into one 8-layer convolutional network, with dropout for regularisation and simple data augmentation (random crops, flips). Proof that depth + scale + data beats hand-engineered features.

    Influenced: ImageNet top-5 error fell from 26.2% to 15.3% overnight; the whole deep-learning explosion followed — VGG (2014), Inception (2014), ResNet (2015, the canonical deep-CNN representative), U-Net (2015), up to ViT for images and the transformer era for everything else. The "scale is the bitter lesson" era started here.

  3. 2013

    word2vec

    Tomáš Mikolov et al., "Efficient Estimation of Word Representations…".

    Motivation: produce dense vector representations of words from co-occurrence statistics — fast enough to train on a billion-word corpus.

    Influenced: the canonical non-contextual word embedding. GloVe (2014) and fastText (2016) are direct descendants; the "words as points in ℝ^d" intuition behind every modern NLP model. See Transformer §2 embeddings.

  4. 2013

    Variational autoencoder

    Diederik P. Kingma & Max Welling (Dec 2013); Rezende, Mohamed & Wierstra independently (2014).

    Motivation: train a latent-variable generative model end-to-end with backpropagation — the reparameterisation trick made it differentiable.

    Influenced: the canonical likelihood-based latent-variable generator. DDPM's denoising is a VAE with a very deep hierarchy of latents; β-VAE, VQ-VAE, and normalising flows are direct descendants. See Diffusion §2 VAE.

  5. 2013

    Deep Q-Network (DQN)

    Volodymyr Mnih et al. (DeepMind), "Playing Atari with Deep Reinforcement Learning" (NIPS Workshop 2013); extended in Nature 2015 as "Human-level control through deep reinforcement learning".

    Motivation: learn a Q-function with a deep CNN directly from raw pixels — an end-to-end RL agent that plays 49 Atari games at human level without hand-engineered features. Two stability tricks made it trainable: experience replay and a slow-moving target network.

    Influenced: the canonical deep value-based agent. Double DQN (2015), Dueling DQN (2016), Rainbow (2017) are direct extensions; the "deep net + replay buffer + target net" template reappears in SAC and TD3 continuous-control. DQN's success was part of what triggered AlphaGo (2016).

  6. 2014

    Generative adversarial network

    Ian Goodfellow et al., NeurIPS 2014.

    Motivation: train a generator by pitting it against a discriminator — an adversarial game that bypasses likelihood altogether.

    Influenced: the canonical implicit generator. DCGAN (Radford et al., 2015) gave the template; StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019) became the photorealism exemplar. Largely supplanted by diffusion for high-fidelity image synthesis by 2022.

  7. 2014

    Adam optimizer

    Diederik P. Kingma & Jimmy Ba, "Adam: A Method for Stochastic Optimization".

    Motivation: combine momentum (RMSProp) with per-parameter adaptive learning rates; default hyper-parameters that actually work.

    Influenced: the default optimiser for transformers, diffusion training, almost every modern paper. AdamW (decoupled weight decay, 2019) is the LLM standard.

  8. 2014

    Attention for seq2seq

    Dzmitry Bahdanau, Kyunghyun Cho & Yoshua Bengio, "Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate".

    Motivation: stop forcing a seq2seq model to compress the whole source sentence into one hidden state; let the decoder softly attend to relevant source positions.

    Influenced: the Transformer (2017) took attention and dropped the RNN entirely. Direct ancestor of every LLM. See Transformer §3 Q/K/V.

  9. 2015

    ResNet (residual connections)

    Kaiming He, Xiangyu Zhang, Shaoqing Ren & Jian Sun (Microsoft Research Asia), "Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition", CVPR 2016 best-paper award.

    Motivation: train networks 100+ layers deep without the degradation problem — a puzzle where deeper nets trained worse, not just overfit more. The fix is a skip path: — gradients can now flow past any sub-block that's still learning.

    Influenced: the canonical deep-CNN representative — ResNet-50 is still the most-used image backbone nine years on. Its "x + sublayer(x)" residual pattern recurs in every transformer block (Transformer §7), DenseNet, and U-Net (used as the denoising backbone in diffusion models).

  10. 2016

    AlphaGo

    David Silver et al. (DeepMind), Nature 2016.

    Motivation: beat a professional Go player — the long-assumed "20 years away" benchmark — by combining MCTS with deep policy and value networks.

    Influenced: the canonical "search + learned prior" agent. AlphaGo Zero (2017) dropped the supervised-learning bootstrap; AlphaZero (2018) generalised to chess and shogi; MuZero (2019) learned the environment model itself. Same template in AlphaFold 2 and LLM theorem-provers today. See RL §8 MCTS.

Era 5 — Transformer era

2017 onward

One architecture — a stack of attention + MLP blocks — took over language, then vision, then audio, then protein folding. The interesting action moved to scaling, data curation, and alignment. Diffusion models emerged in parallel and ate image/video generation.

  1. 2017

    Transformer

    Vaswani et al. (Google Brain), "Attention Is All You Need".

    Motivation: get rid of the recurrent bottleneck in seq2seq — attend to the whole input in parallel with multi-head attention + feed-forward blocks.

    Influenced: BERT, GPT, T5, ViT, Whisper, AlphaFold — essentially every model people talk about today. The architecture in Transformer Lab.

  2. 2017

    Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)

    John Schulman et al. (OpenAI).

    Motivation: a simpler, more stable policy-gradient method than TRPO — clip the ratio of new-to-old policies to prevent destructive updates.

    Influenced: robotics RL, RLHF fine-tuning of LLMs (InstructGPT / ChatGPT), Atari-benchmark standard. The default policy-grad update in RL §7.

  3. 2018

    BERT

    Jacob Devlin, Ming-Wei Chang, Kenton Lee & Kristina Toutanova (Google).

    Motivation: pre-train a bidirectional encoder on masked-language-modelling, then fine-tune on downstream tasks — beat task-specific models by a margin.

    Influenced: the canonical encoder-only transformer. RoBERTa (2019), DistilBERT, DeBERTa, ModernBERT are descendants; sentence-embedding and dense-retrieval models (Sentence-BERT, ColBERT) inherit the bidirectional-attention + mask-prediction recipe. See Transformer §9.

  4. 2018

    GPT (original)

    Alec Radford, Karthik Narasimhan, Tim Salimans & Ilya Sutskever (OpenAI).

    Motivation: same pre-train-then-fine-tune recipe as BERT, but decoder-only with a next-token-prediction objective.

    Influenced: the canonical decoder-only transformer — every modern LLM (GPT-2/3/4, Llama, Mistral, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek) is in this family. See Transformer §8 no-peeking rule.

  5. 2019

    T5 — Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer

    Colin Raffel et al. (Google), "Exploring the Limits of Transfer Learning with a Unified Text-to-Text Transformer", JMLR 2020 (arXiv Oct 2019).

    Motivation: cast every NLP task — translation, summarisation, classification, QA — as a text-to-text problem on the original encoder-decoder transformer. Single model, single loss, one architecture to rule them all.

    Influenced: the canonical encoder-decoder transformer. BART (Lewis et al., 2019) is the sibling; Whisper inherits the architecture for audio → text; instruction-tuning and seq2seq RLHF build on the text-to-text framing. See Transformer §9 families.

  6. 2020

    DDPM

    Jonathan Ho, Ajay Jain & Pieter Abbeel, "Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models".

    Motivation: refine Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) diffusion models by training the network to predict the noise directly — simple loss, beat GANs on image quality.

    Influenced: the canonical diffusion recipe. DDIM (2020, deterministic sampling); classifier-free guidance (2021); Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022 — the canonical product, latent-space diffusion with a U-Net backbone); DALL-E 2; SDXL; video generators. See Diffusion §4.

  7. 2020

    GPT-3 & scaling laws

    Tom Brown et al. (OpenAI); Kaplan et al. for the accompanying scaling-law paper.

    Motivation: test whether scaling up model + data + compute keeps improving the loss — and whether emergent abilities (few-shot in-context learning) appear at scale.

    Influenced: the canonical foundation model — triggered the whole "foundation-model" framing; Chinchilla compute-optimal scaling (2022), Llama 2/3, DeepSeek, the modern LLM arms race. Few-shot in-context learning as emergent ability was the key empirical surprise.

  8. 2020

    Vision Transformer (ViT)

    Alexey Dosovitskiy et al. (Google), "An Image is Worth 16x16 Words", ICLR 2021 (arXiv Oct 2020).

    Motivation: test whether the transformer block — with no convolutional inductive bias — can match CNNs on image classification when pre-trained on enough data. Treat each image as a sequence of 16×16 patches, linearly projected into tokens.

    Influenced: the canonical transformer for images; beat ResNet on ImageNet given enough pre-training data. DeiT, Swin, DINO, CLIP's image tower, and modern multimodal models (Gemini, GPT-4V) all inherit the patches-as-tokens recipe. Key datum behind the "transformers win every modality given enough data" story.

  9. 2021

    Score SDE & continuous-time diffusion

    Yang Song et al., "Score-Based Generative Modeling through Stochastic Differential Equations".

    Motivation: unify DDPM and score matching by treating the forward/reverse diffusion as an SDE; the probability-flow ODE gave deterministic sampling.

    Influenced: modern diffusion sampler variants, latent diffusion (Rombach et al. 2021), flow matching. See Diffusion §6 SDE view.

  10. 2021

    Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE)

    Jianlin Su et al., "RoFormer: Enhanced Transformer with Rotary Position Embedding".

    Motivation: encode relative position inside Q and K multiplicatively (rotating in feature space) rather than adding sinusoidal PE to embeddings — extrapolates better to long contexts.

    Influenced: Llama, Mistral, Gemini, almost every LLM trained after 2022. Named in Transformer §6.

  11. 2022

    InstructGPT / ChatGPT

    Long Ouyang et al. (OpenAI); ChatGPT launched November 2022.

    Motivation: align a base LLM to follow instructions by combining supervised fine-tuning with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) using PPO as the optimiser.

    Influenced: Claude (2023), Gemini, open-source instruct fine-tunes, RLAIF, DPO, the entire chat-assistant product category.

  12. 2023

    Flow matching

    Yaron Lipman, Ricky T. Q. Chen, Heli Ben-Hamu, Maximilian Nickel & Matt Le.

    Motivation: train continuous normalising flows by regressing onto an analytic target velocity field — simpler, faster-sampling alternative to diffusion.

    Influenced: rectified flow (Liu et al., 2022), Stable Diffusion 3, audio / molecular generative models. The current frontier of continuous-time generative modelling.

Caveats. "Who invented X" is almost always contested. Where credit splits, I've picked the most widely cited primary paper and named co-inventors explicitly. Dates are publication years — many ideas were in the air for years or decades before the canonical citation. If you spot an error, the fix is one HTML card.
