In plain English — what is a transformer, really?

Imagine you're reading a mystery novel. When you hit the word "she", your brain instinctively glances back to the most recent female character and connects the two. Transformers learn to do the same thing for every word: for each one, they figure out which other words in the sentence matter most, then mix in meaning from those words.

That mixing step is called attention, and this lab lets you watch it happen step by step — tokenize a sentence, see each word's three personality vectors, watch every word score every other word, and see the final blended output.

Six words that keep coming up:

  • Token — a chunk the model reads. Usually a word, sometimes part of a word. Transformers can't read letters the way you do; they work on pre-chopped pieces.
  • Embedding — a list of numbers that stands in for a token. Lets the computer do math on meaning.
  • Query / Key / Value (Q · K · V) — three "views" every token has. Query = "what am I looking for?"; Key = "what do I advertise?"; Value = "what do I actually carry?"
  • Attention — how much each token listens to each other token. One number per token-pair.
  • Softmax — a recipe that turns raw scores into percentages that sum to 100%. Makes the attention weights easy to interpret.
  • Context vector — the final output per token. It's the token's value blended with every other token's value, weighted by how much attention the token paid.

Classroom analogy: every kid in class has a question on their mind (Q), wears a nametag (K), and carries a backpack full of stuff (V). A question looks around the room: "whose nametag matches what I want?" The best matches get to empty their backpack into yours. Whatever ends up in your backpack is your new, smarter version of yourself.

Step 2 — each token becomes three vectors

Kid version: think of each token as a kid with three personality cards.
🟡 Q — Query: what question is this kid trying to answer?
🔵 K — Key: what does this kid advertise about themself? (their nametag)
🟢 V — Value: what stuff does this kid actually carry? (their backpack)
The cards are made by running the token's embedding (a list of numbers) through three fixed recipes. Each bar below is one slot of one card.

Every token's embedding e is multiplied by three fixed matrices to produce Q (query — what am I looking for?), K (key — what do I advertise?) and V (value — what do I carry?).

Q = e · WQ  ·  K = e · WK  ·  V = e · WV

Step 3 — every query scores every key

Kid version: every kid's Query walks around the room and asks "does your nametag (Key) match what I want?" High match → score is big. Low match → score is small. Then we use softmax — a trick that turns the raw scores into percentages that sum to 100%. That tells each kid exactly how much of each classmate to listen to. In the heatmap below, each ROW is one kid's listening plan: bright squares = "I'm paying a lot of attention to that kid".

Score sij = Qi · Kj / √d. The softmax across each row turns those scores into weights that sum to 1 — those are the attention weights.

Hover or click a row to see which tokens that query ends up blending. Brighter cells = higher weight.

Step 4 — blend the value vectors

Kid version: remember each kid's Value was their backpack? Now the kids whose listening plan said "55% Alice, 30% Bob, 15% Cara" dump 55% of Alice's backpack, 30% of Bob's, 15% of Cara's into one new backpack. That mixed backpack is this kid's context vector — their fresh, context-aware representation. Do this for every token and you're done: one transformer block, no magic, just weighted sums.

The context vector for token i is a weighted sum of the value vectors from every other token, using the attention weights from Step 3.

outputi = Σj αij · Vj

Transformer theory tour

1 · Tokens — chopping text into model-edible pieces

Kid version. A printer can't eat a whole novel — it needs paper sliced into sheets. A transformer can't eat a whole sentence — it needs text sliced into tokens. A token is usually a word, sometimes a piece of a word, and always an integer once the model looks at it. The word "unbelievability" is rare, so a real tokenizer splits it into familiar chunks like [un, believ, abil, ity] — four tokens — so the model can still understand it from its parts.

The demo above uses a simple word-level split with [CLS] / [SEP] sentinels. Production models use subword tokenizers (BPE, WordPiece, SentencePiece) that learn a vocabulary from training data — common words stay whole, rare words decompose into pieces already in the vocabulary. Vocab size is usually 30k–100k.

Practice problem. A BPE tokenizer knows the pieces {pizza, trans, form, ing, ly, un, believ, able}. How many tokens is "transforming pizza"? How about "unbelievably"? Answers: 4 tokens (trans · form · ing · pizza) and 4 tokens (un · believ · able · ly). This is why "GPT charges per token" — long words cost more.

2 · Embeddings — meaning as a list of numbers

Kid version. Imagine every kid has a secret profile card with, say, six numbers on it: how loud they are, how much they love pizza, how good at soccer, how shy, how tall, how old. Kids with similar cards become friends; kids with very different cards don't hang out. Words work the same — the model turns each token into a list of numbers (its embedding), and two words with similar numbers are treated as similar words.

An embedding is a learned lookup from token id → vector in . For small models dmodel is ~128; GPT-3 uses 12288. Two embeddings' cosine similarity (their dot product, normalised) is how "close in meaning" they are to the model.

Practice problem. With cat = (0.9, 0.8), kitten = (0.85, 0.82), algorithm = (−0.7, 0.3) — which is closer to cat, kitten or algorithm? Compute cosine similarity. Answer: kitten by a mile. The famous identity (Mikolov et al., 2013) works because embeddings learn that "royal" and "gender" live on different directions in the vector space.

3 · Query, Key, Value — three lenses on the same token

Kid version. Think of a library. Your Query is what you're looking for: "books about dragons." Each book's Key is its spine — what it advertises to the outside world. Its Value is what's actually inside. You scan spines (Keys) against your search (Query), pick the ones that match, and read their contents (Values). A transformer does this for every token in parallel.

Starting from an embedding , three learned projections produce three views:

Why three? Because "what I'm asking" and "what I advertise" and "what I can deliver" are three different roles. WQ, WK, WV are learned during training — the model discovers the best lenses for the task. Dimensions: typically Q and K have the same width dk; V can be a different width dv.

Pronoun-resolution walk-through. In "The cat sat on the mat because it was tired", the token it's Query ideally encodes "singular subject I need to refer back to". The Keys of earlier tokens advertise what they are — cat's Key says "animal, singular, subject"; mat's says "object, singular, inanimate". Query matches cat's Key best, so the model pulls in cat's Value. That's how transformers resolve pronouns without hard-coded rules.

4 · Scaled dot-product attention — score, blend, repeat

Kid version. Every kid's Query walks around the room, compares itself to every other kid's Key, and gets a raw score per classmate. Then all of one kid's scores go into a softmax blender — it turns the raw scores into percentages that add up to 100%. Those percentages tell the kid how much of each classmate's backpack (Value) to pour into their own. The new mixed-backpack is the kid's context-aware self.

For the whole sequence at once:

Why divide by √dk? If dk is large (say 512), random dot products have variance dk — the scores get huge, softmax saturates to nearly one-hot, gradients vanish. √dk normalises the variance back to 1 so softmax stays differentiable. Not a decoration — this one line makes the model trainable.

Worked example, dk = 2, three tokens. Query Q1 = (1, 0). Keys K1 = (1, 0), K2 = (0, 1), K3 = (0.7, 0.7). Raw scores: 1·1=1, 1·0=0, 1·0.7=0.7. Divide by √2: (0.71, 0, 0.50). Exponentiate: (2.03, 1.00, 1.65). Normalise (softmax): (0.43, 0.21, 0.35). Token 1 "listens" 43% to itself, 21% to token 2, 35% to token 3 — and its output is 0.43·V1 + 0.21·V2 + 0.35·V3. The live demo at the top of this page is running this same arithmetic on whatever sentence you type.

5 · Multi-head attention — eight kids each watching something different

Kid version. One pair of eyes can't track everything at once. So we have eight kids in the room, and each watches a different thing — one tracks who's friends with whom, one tracks what people are wearing, one tracks who's talking first. They each write down their attention scores, then we staple their reports together into one thick report. That's multi-head attention: h parallel attentions, each with its own Q/K/V projection, each learning to focus on a different pattern.

What the heads actually learn. Clark et al. (2019) analysed BERT's heads and found specialists: one head almost always attends to the next token, one to the previous, one to the [CLS] sentinel, one to syntactic dependents (subjects → their verbs), one to coreferents (pronouns → their antecedents). Nobody told the model to do this; each head picked up a role during training because the loss rewarded it. h = 8 or 12 is typical; GPT-4 reportedly uses 96 heads per layer.

6 · Positional encoding — teaching order to a bag of words

Kid version. Attention has no sense of order on its own — without help, it'd read "dog bites man" exactly the same way as "man bites dog". Disaster. So we stamp every token with a position signature — a unique "time tag" made of sine and cosine waves spinning at different speeds. Like a clock with a fast seconds hand, a medium minutes hand, and a slow hour hand: no two moments have the same combined reading. The model learns to decode the signature and figure out "I'm the third word."

The 100002i/d denominator gives each dimension a different "clock speed": low-i dimensions spin fast (resolve short distances); high-i dimensions spin slowly (resolve long ones). The resulting vector is added to each token's embedding. Modern LLMs use RoPE (rotary position encoding) — the same idea applied multiplicatively inside Q and K instead of added to the embedding; it extrapolates better to long contexts.

7 · The Transformer block — attention, FFN, residuals, LayerNorm

Kid version. One classroom discussion isn't enough to fully understand anything. Between group talks, every kid also gets one-on-one tutoring with their own private teacher (the feed-forward network). Everyone always keeps a copy of their old self (the residual connection, in case the new ideas turn out to be wrong). And the teacher makes sure nobody shouts or whispers too much — everyone's contribution stays at a reasonable volume (layer normalisation). Then the whole thing repeats, layer after layer. Depth = many classrooms in a row.

Typical depths. BERT-base has N = 12, BERT-large N = 24. GPT-2 has N = 12 (small) to 48 (XL). GPT-3 has N = 96. Llama-70B has N = 80. More layers = more refinement passes; each layer roughly re-asks "given what we know so far, what should every token mean?"

8 · Encoder, decoder, and the no-peeking rule

Kid version. Three flavours of transformer, depending on how they use the block above. A BERT-style librarian reads the whole sentence at once — every token sees every other token — and understands. A GPT-style speaker generates a word at a time and must not peek at the future (otherwise it'd just copy the answer). A T5-style translator reads one language fully, then writes another word by word. The no-peeking rule for GPT is called causal masking: we set all "look-at-future" attention scores to −∞ so softmax makes them exactly 0.

In a decoder-only model generating position i, attention scores for j > i are masked out before softmax, giving an upper-triangular zero pattern in the attention matrix:

Why the no-peek rule matters. During training we feed the whole sentence at once and compute all output positions in parallel. Without the mask, predicting "pizza" at position 3 of "I love pizza" could just look at position 3 and output itself — training would learn nothing. With the mask, position 3's attention sees only positions 0–2, so the model has to actually predict. At inference, a GPT runs one token at a time, so the mask is moot — but training needs it.

9 · Transformer families — a clustering map

Kid version. Three big families of transformer, split by who they talk to and what they predict. Encoder-only models read but don't speak (librarians). Decoder-only models speak one word at a time (autoregressive storytellers). Encoder-decoder models read then speak — one language in, another out (translators, summarisers). The same block from §7 powers all three; the difference is which attention masks you allow and which loss you train against.

Three cross-cutting axes you'll see in every paper: (1) directionality — bidirectional vs causal attention; (2) training objective — masked- token vs next-token vs denoising / seq2seq; (3) positional scheme — learned, sinusoidal, relative (T5), or RoPE (most new LLMs). Every other difference — tokenizer, hidden size, head count, layer count, activation choice — tunes performance but doesn't change the family.
