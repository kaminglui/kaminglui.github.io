Read this first

  1. What it does A diffusion model learns to reverse a fixed noising process. The forward process gradually adds Gaussian noise to data over T steps; the reverse process learns to remove that noise one step at a time. Generating a new sample = sampling pure noise, then iteratively denoising it into something that looks like data.

  3. Look for this At large t the score ≈ −x (everything points toward origin — pure Gaussian); at small t the score sharpens and points toward the nearest mode. This is why diffusion must take many small steps: the score is only reliable locally.

In plain English — what is a diffusion model?

Imagine a photograph dropped in a snowstorm. Each second a little more snow sticks to it until eventually it's just a white rectangle. That's the forward process — easy, mechanical, always the same. A diffusion model learns the reverse: given a snowed-over photo, guess what the photo looked like one second ago. Do that a thousand times in a row and you've removed all the snow.

Generating a new photo is the magic trick: start with pure snow (an all-white rectangle ≈ Gaussian noise) and apply the same "rewind one second" network over and over. The network has never seen this noise before — but because the forward process always turns data into Gaussian noise, the reverse is conditioned to turn Gaussian noise into data.

The particles below are 2D "pixels": x, y coordinates instead of RGB channels. The target is a small Gaussian mixture you can see as faint circles on the canvas. Everything works identically in 3 million dimensions (a 1024×1024 image) — we just can't draw that.

Words that show up a lot:

  • Latent variable — a hidden random variable that generates the data. In VAEs it's z; in diffusion it's every noisy xt.
  • Noise schedule — the pre-chosen sequence β1, …, βT saying how much noise to add each step. Fixed; not learned.
  • Score — the gradient of log-density, ∇x log p(x). Points from "unlikely" regions toward "more likely" ones.
  • ELBO — Evidence Lower BOund on log p(x). VAEs and diffusion both maximise it.
  • KL divergence — "distance" from distribution p to q. Not symmetric: KL(p‖q) ≠ KL(q‖p), and that asymmetry matters a lot in practice.
  • Reparameterisation trick — write a sample as z = μ + σε with ε ~ 𝒩(0,I) so gradients flow through μ and σ.
  • SDE — stochastic differential equation. Diffusion is cleanest in the continuous-time SDE view.

Diffusion theory tour

1 · KL divergence: the tape measure between distributions

Kid version. You have two guesses for where stuff lives: yours and the truth. KL divergence is a number that gets bigger the more your guess misses places the truth cares about. It's not symmetric: missing a peak costs differently depending on whose side you read it from.

  • p — the "reference" distribution (the one the expectation is taken under).
  • q — the model being compared against p.
  • DKL ≥ 0 with equality iff p = q almost everywhere. Not a metric (not symmetric, no triangle inequality).

Two orderings matter. In ML, the same symbols flip meaning depending on which side you put data:

Forward KL — DKL(pdata ‖ qθ)Reverse KL — DKL(qθ ‖ p)
Minimises to Maximum likelihood: Variational inference: model ducks low-probability regions of p
Behaviour Mean-seeking / mass-covering — qθ spreads over every mode of p Mode-seeking — qθ locks onto one mode, ignores the rest
Who uses it Normalising flows, autoregressive LLMs, diffusion (ELBO) Classic VI, some VAE variants (reverse-KL formulation)

Diffusion training minimises a forward-KL on the joint of noisy variables — not a single DKL(pdata ‖ qθ), but a sum of per-step KLs between q(xt−1|xt, x0) and pθ(xt−1|xt). Each term is a Gaussian-vs-Gaussian KL, which collapses to a closed-form L2 loss. See §4.

2 · VAE: the ancestor of diffusion

Kid version. Imagine compressing a photo to a short code, then building the photo back from the code. A variational autoencoder does this but with randomness: each photo maps to a fuzzy ball in code-space, and decoding samples from that ball. The fuzzy ball is what lets you later sample new photos by just grabbing a random code.

A VAE has three pieces:

  • Encoder — maps data x to a distribution over latents z (usually Gaussian with learned mean and variance).
  • Decoder — maps a latent z back to a distribution over data.
  • Prior — a simple distribution we can sample.

3 · Random walk → Brownian motion → forward diffusion

Kid version. Flip a coin: step left or right. Do it a thousand times. Your position spreads out in a bell shape. Make the steps tiny and fast and that's Brownian motion. Do the same thing to every pixel of a photograph and that's the forward diffusion process.

Add a drift f(x, t) and state-dependent diffusion g(t) and you get an Itô SDE:

  • f(x, t) — drift: deterministic nudge at each instant.
  • g(t) — diffusion coefficient: scale of the random kicks.
  • dW — Brownian increment, 𝒩(0, dt) in each dimension.

Diffusion's forward process is exactly this SDE, discretised. The drift pulls x toward zero (so the marginal doesn't explode), the diffusion coefficient adds noise. Different choices of (f, g) give the VP and VE flavours in §6.

4 · DDPM: the Ho/Jain/Abbeel recipe

Kid version. Each step blurs the picture a tiny bit with Gaussian snow. Training means: pick a random t, show the network "here's the picture with t snowstorms worth of snow — guess the snow." The network learns to predict the noise.

5 · Score matching and Langevin sampling

Kid version. Instead of teaching the net "what does data look like?", teach it "which way is uphill on the data distribution?" — the score. Then climb that hill while adding a little noise so you don't get stuck.

Langevin dynamics turns a score into samples:

  • η — step size for the Langevin climb (small is accurate, slow).
  • K — number of Langevin iterations.
  • zk — fresh Gaussian noise at each step; keeps the chain exploring instead of getting stuck at a local maximum.

As η → 0 and K → ∞, xK is distributed according to p. Song & Ermon (2019) showed that running Langevin at decreasing noise scales (annealed SMLD) produces high-quality samples — and that this is mathematically equivalent to DDPM up to parameterisation:

Two sides of one coin. "Predict the noise" and "predict the score" are the same object wearing different scalars. The interactive above uses the closed-form score of the GMM — every denoising step is one stochastic Langevin climb at the current noise level.

6 · The SDE view: VP, VE, and the probability-flow ODE

Kid version. Take DDPM and make the steps infinitely small. You get a stochastic differential equation that you can reverse in closed form. Two popular choices differ only in how they balance "shrink the signal" vs "add noise."

Song et al. (2021) showed that both DDPM and SMLD are discretisations of a continuous-time forward SDE:

And — using Anderson's (1982) time-reversal identity — the reverse SDE is:

  • dW̄ — Brownian increment for time running backward (T → 0). Independent of the forward dW; fresh randomness for the reverse integration.
  • pt(x) — marginal density at time t under the forward SDE.

The only unknown is the score ∇ log pt. Train sθ once; integrate this backward to generate.

VP-SDE (DDPM)

  • Variance Preserving: marginal stays bounded.
  • Continuous version of DDPM.

VE-SDE (SMLD)

  • Variance Exploding: marginal variance grows to σmax².
  • Continuous version of annealed Langevin / SMLD.

Probability-flow ODE

  • Deterministic. Same marginals as the SDE.
  • Enables exact likelihood, fast ODE solvers (DPM-Solver, Heun).

The PF-ODE is why a 1000-step DDPM can be compressed to 20–50 ODE-solver steps with almost no quality loss — the stochastic dW doesn't carry sampling information, only diversity.

7 · Modern tricks — guidance, DDIM, latent, flow matching, consistency

Kid version. The five ideas that took diffusion from "cute 2019 MNIST demo" to "Stable Diffusion / Veo / Sora in production."

Classifier-free guidance

  • c — conditioning (text prompt, class label, image mask).
  • ∅ — "null" condition; a learned embedding meaning "no condition." Model sees it ≈10 % of training.
  • w — guidance scale; w=0 is conditional only, w→∞ over-saturates.
  • Trick: train one model to predict ε under either c or ∅; extrapolate at inference.

DDIM (non-Markov)

  • x̂0 — model's current guess of the clean data: x̂0 = (xt − √(1−ᾱt) εθ) / √ᾱt.
  • Deterministic (σ=0) sampling path — no fresh noise added.
  • Skip steps; 1000 → 50 inference steps with the same training loss as DDPM.

Latent diffusion (SD, Imagen)

  • Pre-train a VAE that maps 512×512 images to 64×64 latents.
  • Run diffusion in latent space — 64× fewer FLOPs per step.
  • Unlocks text-to-image at consumer GPU scale.

Flow matching

  • x0 — a real data sample. x1 — a fresh noise sample ~ 𝒩(0, I). xt linearly interpolates between them.
  • vθ — predicted velocity field dx/dt along the path. Ideal target is the straight-line velocity x1 − x0.
  • No noise schedule; ODE-only sampling. Stable Diffusion 3, Flux, Veo use this.

Consistency models

  • fθ — the consistency function. Trained so every point on a PF-ODE trajectory maps to the same x0.
  • θ⁻ — EMA (exponential moving average) of θ; acts as a slowly-updated target model, like in DQN / SAC.
  • 1-step sampling (with small quality loss) or 2–4 step. LCM, SDXL-Turbo, ADD.
